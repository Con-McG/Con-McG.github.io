Understanding Number Systems

Decimal, Binary, and Hexadecimal

What Are Number Systems?

Number systems represent numbers in different formats. The most common systems are Decimal, Binary, and Hexadecimal. WE prrmarily use Base-10 because we have ten fingers.

Decimal (Base-10)

The decimal system, or base-10, is the most commonly used number system in everyday life, consisting of ten digits: 0 through 9. Each position in a decimal number represents a power of 10. For example, the number 345 in decimal is 3 × 102 + 4 × 101 + 5 × 100, which equals 345. The decimal system has been in use for thousands of years, with its roots traced back to ancient civilizations such as the Egyptians and Indians, who contributed to its development as a standardized counting method.

Binary (Base-2)

The binary system, or base-2, uses only two digits: 0 and 1, which correspond to the on and off states in digital circuits. Each position in a binary number represents a power of 2. For example, the number 345 in binary is 101011001, which equals 1 × 28 + 0 × 27 + 1 × 26 + 0 × 25 + 1 × 24 + 1 × 23 + 0 × 22 + 0 × 21 + 1 × 20 = 345 in decimal. Binary was formalized in the 17th century by mathematician Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, and became crucial for the development of digital computers in the 20th century.

Hexadecimal (Base-16)

The hexadecimal system, or base-16, uses 16 digits: 0-9 for values 0 to 9 and A-F for values 10 to 15. It offers a more compact representation of binary numbers. For example, the number 345 in hexadecimal is 159, which equals 1 × 162 + 5 × 161 + 9 × 160 = 345 in decimal. Hexadecimal emerged in the 20th century, particularly with the rise of computer science and electronics, because it provides a more human-readable way to express long binary strings, especially in programming and memory addressing.
